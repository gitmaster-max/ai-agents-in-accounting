Audit Perspectives

Every audit question sounds different depending on who you ask. Staff worry about the data. Managers worry about the methodology. Partners worry about the defense file. Regulators worry about solvency. Audit Perspectives runs the question through all of them at once — and maps where they disagree.

Open the live site View the repository

The problem

One question, one pass, one point of view

When an audit team faces a hard question — a judgment-heavy estimate, an unusual transaction, a control that almost works — the answer it gets usually reflects whoever was asked. The staff view never reaches the partner. The regulator's lens never enters the room. The disagreements that matter most stay implicit until review, or worse, until inspection.

Audit Perspectives adapts Stanford's STORM method (Synthesis of Topic Outlines through Retrieval and Multi-perspective Question Asking — Shao et al., NAACL 2024) to the audit profession: instead of researching a topic through multiple expert personas, it interrogates an audit question through every stakeholder who has a genuine stake in the answer.

The method

Four stages, from question to recommendation

1 Topic analysis

Classify the question — technical accounting, audit procedure, risk assessment, control, quality and independence, or regulatory — and pin down the industry context, because a reserving question reads differently in health insurance than in banking.

2 The perspective grid

Generate a distinct viewpoint from every relevant role. Core audit roles — Staff through Partner, plus quality and independent reviewers — are always present. IT Audit, Tax, and Advisory join when the topic touches their domain. External perspectives — PCAOB, AICPA, financial statement users, investors, and the sector regulator — are always included. Redundant perspectives are eliminated, not padded.

3 The tension map

Identify the active conflicts between perspectives and classify each one: operational, risk, regulatory, or incentive-driven. This is the heart of the method — the output isn't a consensus, it's a map of where the room would argue.

4 Synthesis

Consolidate into what everyone agrees on, where the views divide, the regulatory exposure, and a recommendation specific enough to act on — the memo you'd actually want before walking into the partner's office.

Worked example

IBNR reserves, four ways

Ask about incurred-but-not-reported reserve testing at a health insurer and the grid splits immediately:

Internal · Core audit

Staff

Sees the data limitations first — completeness of the claims lag triangles, the inputs nobody upstream wants to talk about.

Internal · Core audit

Manager

Questions whether the methodology is adequate at all — is the development factor approach defensible for this book of business?

Internal · Core audit

Partner

Thinks in terms of audit defense: what does the file need to show if this estimate is challenged two years from now?

External · Regulator

NAIC Regulator

Cares about solvency — whether the reserve protects policyholders, not whether the audit was efficient.

The tension map then surfaces the load-bearing conflict: the staff's informal concerns about judgment-heavy factors haven't reached partner-level discussion. That's not a modeling insight — it's an engagement-management insight, and it's exactly the kind of thing that single-pass answers miss.
